# Foundation Charter

Status: Constitutional
Purpose: Defines Workprint's highest-order principles
Expected Update Frequency: Rarely changes

This charter defines Workprint's constitutional principles. These principles
change rarely. They are meant to outlast individual milestones, interfaces,
adapters, and report formats.

## Evidence Is Primary

Workprint begins with evidence, not conclusions. Every meaningful claim should
be grounded in source material that Workprint can identify and reference.
Evidence may be incomplete, messy, duplicated, or ambiguous, but it remains the
boundary of the investigation.

The system may organize, summarize, and explain evidence. It may not replace
evidence with a more convenient story.

## Unknown Is A Valid Outcome

Unknown is not a failure state. It is often the most accurate answer.

When available evidence does not support a conclusion about authorship,
ownership, intent, effort, decision leadership, or project completeness,
Workprint should say that the answer is unknown. A clear unknown preserves
trust. A confident unsupported claim destroys it.

## Trust Is More Valuable Than Completeness

Workprint should prefer a narrower truthful account over a broader speculative
one. A report that leaves some questions unanswered is stronger than a report
that fills gaps with inference disguised as fact.

Completeness matters only when it is supported. Trust matters always.

## Presentation Never Changes Evidence

Reports, summaries, executive briefs, tables, and JSON outputs are
presentations of investigation data. They may improve readability. They may
change ordering, grouping, labels, and emphasis. They must not alter the
underlying evidence, invent support, or weaken uncertainty.

If a polished report says more than the investigation supports, the report is
wrong no matter how readable it is.

## Every Significant Claim Should Be Traceable

Readers should be able to ask, "Why should I believe this?" and find a clear
path from claim to observation to evidence reference.

Traceability does not require every sentence to carry an inline citation, but
the structure of the report must make support discoverable. Major conclusions,
decision assessments, collaboration patterns, confidence bands, and evidence
gaps should all point back to the material that supports or limits them.

## Human Agency Is Never Fabricated

Workprint may report captured human activity: requests, decisions, reviews,
approvals, edits, comments, questions, and other evidenced actions. It may not
invent human agency to make a project appear more human-led than the evidence
shows.

The system must not convert message counts, edit volume, commits, or tool use
into unsupported claims about ownership, value, effort, or authorship.

## AI Agency Is Never Overstated

Workprint may report captured AI or tool activity when evidence shows it. It
may describe AI-assisted implementation, summarization, generation, analysis,
or review when those activities are supported.

It must not exaggerate AI independence, intent, understanding, or authorship.
AI activity remains bounded by the captured record and the surrounding human
direction visible in that record.

## Investigation Precedes Interpretation

Workprint should first determine what evidence exists, what observations can
be derived, and where boundaries are. Interpretation comes after investigation.

This order protects the product from narrative bias. It also keeps adapters,
normalization, observation extraction, timeline generation, and report writing
in their proper roles.

## Confidence Must Be Explainable

Confidence is not a hidden score. It should be expressed in qualitative bands
with a plain explanation of evidence strength, coverage, corroboration,
conflicts, and gaps.

A reader should understand why confidence is High, Moderate, Limited, or Low.
If the reason cannot be explained, the confidence assessment is not ready.

## Product Decisions Should Increase User Trust

Every major product decision should make Workprint easier to believe. That may
mean clearer evidence links, better unknowns, more careful language, better
source boundaries, or simpler workflows that reduce user error.

Convenience, polish, and narrative quality are valuable only when they support
trust. They must not pressure the system into overclaiming.
